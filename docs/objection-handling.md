# ScopeHouse Objection Handling Sheet v1

## Purpose

This document gives short responses to common objections in discovery calls, demos, and service conversations.

## Response rules

1. Acknowledge the concern.
2. Tie the reply to the control problem.
3. Keep the reply short.
4. Do not force weak-fit leads.

## Price

### Objection
This feels expensive.

### Response
That is fair. The real question is whether the project confusion is already costing enough in time, mistakes, or quote uncertainty to justify getting the record under control now.

### Follow-up
Where would the value need to show up for this to feel worth paying for?

## Trust

### Objection
I am not sure I trust a new tool for something this important.

### Response
That makes sense. ScopeHouse is meant to reduce confusion, not add another layer. The goal is one cleaner record for scope, quotes, decisions, and changes.

### Follow-up
What would you need to see to feel this is grounded enough for your project?

## Early product

### Objection
This looks early.

### Response
That is true. The strongest fit right now is people who want help getting the project set up and are open to shaping the workflow with us.

### Follow-up
Would early access feel more useful if we set the project up with you instead of asking you to figure it out alone?

## Current workflow

### Objection
We already use spreadsheets, notes, or our current system.

### Response
That is common. The real question is whether those records still hold up when quotes, decisions, and changes start moving quickly.

### Follow-up
What part of your current setup gets hardest to trust once the project gets more active?

## Segment mismatch

### Objection
This sounds like it is for someone else, not for me.

### Response
That could be true. ScopeHouse is strongest for people dealing with real renovation control problems, especially around scope, quotes, decisions, and changes.

### Follow-up
Which part feels off, the project size, the workflow, or the kind of problem we are solving?

## Too early

### Objection
We are still early. We are not ready for something like this yet.

### Response
That may be true, but early planning is also where weak scope and scattered decisions start becoming expensive later.

### Follow-up
What would need to happen in the project before this would become useful?

## Too busy

### Objection
I do not have time for another process right now.

### Response
That is exactly when a cleaner record often matters most. If time is the issue, the better path may be for us to help set the project up with you instead of adding work to your plate.

### Follow-up
Would this be easier to consider if the setup work was done with you instead of by you?

## Heavy software

### Objection
I do not want a big contractor system.

### Response
That is exactly the category ScopeHouse is trying to avoid. It is a planning and control layer, not a full contractor ERP or accounting stack.

### Follow-up
What kind of software experience are you trying to avoid?

## AI

### Objection
I do not want AI making project decisions.

### Response
That is not the goal. AI in ScopeHouse is there to support drafting, summarizing, and organizing. The user stays in control of the record and the decisions.

### Follow-up
Would it feel different if AI was framed as support for organization, not as an autopilot?

## No budget

### Objection
We are not spending on this right now.

### Response
Understood. If the problem is not costly enough right now, it may not make sense yet. I would rather be clear on fit than force timing.

### Follow-up
Is the barrier mainly budget, or is it that the problem is not serious enough yet?

## Reminder

A good objection response should clarify:

- whether the pain is real
- whether the timing is real
- whether ScopeHouse fits
- whether the lead should move forward, stay warm, or drop out
