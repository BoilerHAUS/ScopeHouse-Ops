# ScopeHouse Customer Interview Script v1

## Purpose

Create a repeatable interview script for customer discovery and design partner validation.

This script is built to uncover present pain, current workflow, buying signals, and fit.

It is not a demo script.
It is not a pitch deck.
It is a structured conversation guide.

## Primary goals

1. Understand how the person handles renovation planning and control today.
2. Find where scope, budget, quotes, decisions, and changes break down.
3. Confirm whether the problem is active, costly, and urgent.
4. Learn whether ScopeHouse is a fit for their current project reality.
5. Test willingness to pay without asking vague opinion questions.

## Core rules for the interview

1. Ask about current or recent behavior.
2. Avoid future hypotheticals when possible.
3. Stay curious and quiet.
4. Do not rescue the user with answers too early.
5. Let the interviewee describe the mess in their own words.
6. Ask for examples, not summaries.
7. Do not turn the interview into a sales pitch unless they ask to see more.

## Target segments

Primary segment now:

- homeowners planning medium to large renovations

Secondary segment next:

- solo general contractors
- renovation consultants
- small design-build operators

## Interview length

Target:

20 to 30 minutes

## Interview structure

### 1. Opening

Use:

Thanks for taking the time. I’m speaking with people who are actively dealing with renovation planning and project control. I’m trying to understand how people handle scope, budget, quotes, decisions, and changes today, and where things break down.

I’m not here to force a pitch. I want to understand how you do it now.

## 2. Warm-up and context questions

Ask:

- Tell me about the renovation or project you are working on right now.
- What stage is it at today?
- Who is involved in the project?
- How big is the job in practical terms?
- What has felt hardest so far?

What to listen for:

- number of rooms or phases
- number of people involved
- whether the project is already active
- whether the pain is early-planning, quote comparison, or live-job control

## 3. Current workflow questions

Ask:

- How are you keeping track of scope right now?
- How are you handling budget or cost tracking right now?
- How are you comparing quotes or bids today?
- Where do project documents and photos live?
- How do you keep track of decisions that get made?
- When something changes, how do you record it?

Follow with:

- What tools are you using for that?
- Who updates those records?
- What gets missed most often?
- What is spread across too many places?

What to listen for:

- spreadsheets
- notes apps
- text messages
- email threads
- printed documents
- memory-based decision making
- no real system for changes

## 4. Pain questions

Ask:

- Walk me through the last time the project got confusing.
- What was unclear?
- What did that confusion cost you in time, money, or stress?
- Where do quotes become hard to compare?
- What kinds of decisions tend to get lost or revisited?
- What happens when the scope changes after planning starts?
- What part of the project feels least under control today?

Follow with:

- Can you give me a specific example?
- What did you do next?
- Did that solve the problem or only patch it?

What to listen for:

- real examples
- visible cost or delay
- rework
- disputes
- forgotten scope items
- missing approvals
- budget surprises

## 5. Current workaround questions

Ask:

- Have you built any system of your own to deal with this?
- What does that system look like?
- What works well enough today?
- What is frustrating about it?
- Have you tried any software for this before?
- Why did it not stick, or why does it not solve the full problem?

What to listen for:

- partial solutions
- manual workarounds
- why existing tools fail
- resistance to complex software

## 6. Fit and priority questions

Ask:

- If you could fix one part of this process first, what would it be?
- Which is more painful right now: scope clarity, quote comparison, budget control, schedule visibility, or tracking changes?
- How important is this problem compared with everything else on the project?
- Do you need a better system now, or is this more of a nice-to-have?

What to listen for:

- strongest front-door use case
- urgency
- project timing
- whether the pain is active enough for beta or paid pilot fit

## 7. Willingness to pay questions

Do not ask:

Would you pay for this?

Ask instead:

- Have you spent money already to solve part of this problem?
- What have you paid for, if anything?
- If this saved you time, reduced mistakes, or made quote comparison cleaner, where would that value show up for you?
- Who would need to say yes to paying for something like this?
- For a project like yours, would this need to feel like a one-time planning purchase or an ongoing tool?
- If I helped set this up with you and used it on your live project, would that be something you would consider paying for?

For pros, add:

- If this helped you run a cleaner client project, where would the ROI show up first?
- Would you think about this per project, per month, or as part of a client-facing service?

What to listen for:

- existing spend
- budget owner
- pricing model preference
- service openness
- whether pain is strong enough to justify cash

## 8. Optional concept check

Only use this if the conversation shows active pain.

Say:

I can give you the short version of what I’m building and you can tell me whether it matches the problem you’re dealing with.

Then use:

ScopeHouse is a renovation planning and control tool that gives users one clear record for scope, budget, schedule, documents, decisions, and changes. The goal is to help people get control earlier and keep the record clean as the project evolves.

Then ask:

- What part of that feels useful?
- What part feels unnecessary?
- What would have to be true for this to fit your project?
- What is missing from that picture?

## 9. Closing questions

Ask:

- Is there anything important about your process that I did not ask about?
- Do you know anyone else dealing with this kind of renovation complexity?
- Would you be open to a follow-up if I want to test messaging, workflow, or an early version?

If fit is strong, ask:

- Would you be open to being part of an early design partner group?

## 10. Post-call notes to capture

Immediately after the call, log:

- project stage
- top pain point
- current tools used
- strongest broken workflow
- urgency level
- willingness to pay signal
- design partner fit
- follow-up action
- best quote from the call

## Red flags

Be cautious if:

- the project is too small
- the problem is inactive
- the person is only giving polite opinions
- they have no real control over the project
- they have no urgency and no cost tied to the pain
- they want a full contractor ERP or accounting system

## Good signal examples

Strong signal if the interviewee says things like:

- I have everything spread across texts, notes, and spreadsheets.
- I do not have a clean way to compare quotes.
- We keep revisiting decisions because nothing is recorded clearly.
- Changes keep happening but there is no real record.
- I need a better way to organize this before it gets more expensive.

## Interviewer reminder

Your job is to learn where the control problem is real.

Do not chase compliments.
Do not chase vague interest.
Chase pain, urgency, workflow detail, and payment signals.
